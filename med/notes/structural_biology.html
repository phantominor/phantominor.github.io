Chapter 1: Introduction to Structural Biology

Figure 1.1

Study Notes

This figure illustrates the fundamental principles of protein structure hierarchy:

  • Primary structure: The linear sequence of amino acids
  • Secondary structure: Local folding patterns including α-helices and β-sheets
  • Tertiary structure: The overall three-dimensional fold of a single protein chain
  • Quaternary structure: The assembly of multiple protein chains into a functional complex

Key concepts to understand from this figure:

  • Hydrogen bonds stabilize secondary structure elements
  • Hydrophobic interactions drive protein folding
  • Disulfide bridges can covalently link protein regions
  • Salt bridges form between oppositely charged residues

Study Notes

  • X-ray crystallography: Provides high-resolution structures of crystallized proteins
  • NMR spectroscopy: Allows determination of protein structures in solution
  • Cryo-EM: Enables visualization of large macromolecular complexes
Method Resolution Advantages Limitations
X-ray Crystallography ~0.5-3Å High resolution, works well for small-large proteins Requires crystallization, static structures
NMR Spectroscopy ~2-5Å Solution state, dynamic information Size limitations (~30-40 kDa)
Cryo-EM ~2-4Å (can reach ~1.5Å) Works with large complexes, no crystallization Historically lower resolution, improving rapidly

Figure 1.3

Study Notes

This figure shows the relationship between structure and function in proteins:

  • Binding sites: Structural pockets that recognize specific molecules
  • Catalytic sites: Regions where enzyme reactions occur
  • Conformational changes: Structural rearrangements during protein function

Important functional considerations:

  • Shape complementarity is essential for molecular recognition
  • Electrostatic interactions often guide initial binding events
  • Induced fit mechanism allows proteins to adjust around binding partners
  • Allosteric regulation involves structural changes at sites distant from binding

Figure 2.2

Study Notes - Part A

This figure illustrates secondary structure elements in proteins:

  • Alpha helix: Spiral arrangement stabilized by hydrogen bonds
  • Key features: 3.6 residues per turn, hydrogen bonds between i and i+4 residues
  • Helix dipole: Net positive charge at N-terminus, negative at C-terminus

Study Notes - Part B

Continuation of secondary structure elements:

  • Beta sheet: Extended strands connected by hydrogen bonds
  • Parallel sheets: Strands running in the same direction
  • Antiparallel sheets: Strands running in opposite directions
  • Beta turns: Connect consecutive beta strands, often contain glycine or proline

The Ramachandran plot shows allowed phi (φ) and psi (ψ) angles in protein backbones:

  • Alpha helices cluster around φ = -60°, ψ = -45°
  • Beta sheets cluster around φ = -120°, ψ = +130°
  • Left-handed alpha helices are rare due to steric hindrance
  • Glycine shows wider distribution due to lack of side chain
